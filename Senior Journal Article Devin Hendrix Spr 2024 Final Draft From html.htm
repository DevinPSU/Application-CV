Characterizing the PilZ Domain Protein, PA0012, and Its Coeffectors, ChpB and PilH Necessary for Directed

Twitching Motility of Pseudomonas Aeruginosa Towards Staphylococcus Aureus

Devin Hendrix, Nicholas Hammons, Ernesto Fuentes

Abstract

PilZ domain proteins are important to the virulence and motility of Pseudomonas aeruginosa.

These proteins have been proven to be integral to the Type IV Directed Twitching Motility Secretion System (Yarrington et al., 2022) and are valuable in learning about potentially targetable regions of the Pil-Chp pathway present in the system. These targets within this pathway could be exploited for use in antibiotic development.

Here we purified and observed a coeffector protein from the Type IV system of Pseudomonas aeruginosa which, through a bacterial two hybrid assay (Yarrington et al., 2022), has been shown to have the potential for binding to an integral PilZ domain protein, PA0012. Using PA0012 and cyclic di gmp (cdG) in complex, we used pull-down assays to ascertain the validity of these proposed coeffector proteins. We were able to identify the binding coefficient of the PA0012:cdG complex and were able to successfully bind this complex to one of its identified potential binding partners, PilH.

We were unable to ascertain these values from ChpB due to time constraints and the volatility of the protein as it rapidly degraded when stored at 4C. We theorize this could be slowed or avoided if stored at -20C or lower. However, due to the 10-week time limit and the need for a phosphorylation assay before more testing of ChpB could be conducted, it was not the primary coeffector focused on in this study. Future studies of ChpB will hopefully be fruitful in determining the effect of phosphorylation on its structure and function, as well as its potential for binding with the protein complex PA0012:cdg.

Introduction

Bacteria exist on nearly every surface, crevasse, and corner of Earth. The bacterial world is vast and involves the interaction between many distinct species to create an ecosystem capable of supporting life and thriving. Many species, including humans, have survived by hosting bacterial species beneficial for our health and necessary for certain metabolic functions. However, some bacteria are harmful and dangerous for the organism in which they reside. Of these malignant bacteria, some are capable of working together symbiotically to bolster the infectious agents and toxins present in the other through a process called coinfection. These toxins and tools which bacteria can use to arm themselves against their hosts defenses or against other bacterias toxins are referred to as virulence factors. These bacteria participating in coinfection can share a host without harming one another whilst also benefiting each other. Through coinfection, virulence can be increased, and in the system of Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus, we observed the specific proteins present in an important part of the coinfection mechanism of Pseudomonas aeruginosa.

A coinfection is when two or more bacterial agents are inhabiting a host at a time and where each bacteria affects one anothers virulence. This is hypothesized to be caused by the need for an increase of competitive virulence factors and is influenced by the number of competing strains and their strain-specific interactions with one another (Kinnula et al., 2017). The virulence of a bacterial strain holds importance in clinical settings as increased virulence has been shown to lead to more lethal and more resistant bacterial strains making them more difficult to cure and lowering the survivability of infected hosts. These virulence factors can come in many different forms, (Sharma et al., 2017), namely those secretory, membrane associated, or cytosolic in nature. These factors have different effects and advantages based on the environment the infectious agent inhabits (intracellular vs extracellular), and the situation it needs to adapt to (Kroken, 2023; Bravo-Santano, 2018). Therefore, studying coinfection is important to understanding the way microbial community members interact and collaborate with one another in an environment. The bacteria associated with our study, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, has many different virulence factors, a veritable toolbelt of weaponry built for adapting to different environments. Its Type VI Secretion System, for example, has a needle-like shape capable of puncturing cell membranes and secreting toxins (usually Pseudomonas aeruginosa DNA) directly into the cytosol of the attacked cell (Hachani, 2011; Tashiro, 2013). This is especially effective against prokaryotes as their transcription and translation occur in the cytosol meaning the secreted DNA does not have to pass through the nucleus before being translated. This virulence factor causes great damage to host cells and is an important part of the weaponry Pseudomonas aeruginosa holds.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a well-studied bacteria commonly found in the lungs of cystic fibrosis patients. Cystic fibrosis is one of the best-studied chronic respiratory [infections] and has been used to study Pseudomonas aeruginosa infections since 1946 (Limoli and Hoffman, 2019). For these reasons, it was chosen to test coinfection as cystic fibrosis is commonly known to be an optimal environment for several other bacterial species too, namely Staphylococcus aureus. The way Staphylococcus aureus and Pseudomonas aeruginosa interact directly affects their ability to survive in patients with cystic fibrosis. Pseudomonas aeruginosa has been shown to affect the expression and regulation of three of the virulence factors secreted by

Staphylococcus aureus. Namely, Panton-Valentine Leukocidin (PVL), alpha-haemolysin (Hla) and S. aureus protein A (SpA) (Limoli and Hoffman, 2019), of which SpA is the most important and is directly involved in the Type IV Twitching Motility Secretion System (T4SS) of Pseudomonas aeruginosa studied in our paper. Staphylococcus aureus is also thought to increase the expression of several virulence factors present in Pseudomonas aeruginosa, namely Pyocyanin, Elastase, and some anti-Staphylococcal factors which are anti-proteinase inhibitors. These virulence factors combined cause massive damage to the host cell at minimal cost to the infectious agents.

The T4SS of Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the most important virulence factor for its interaction with Staphylococcus aureus. This is because the T4SS of Pseudomonas aeruginosa allows it to get close enough to Staphylococcus aureus for their inter-species interaction to occur. Staphylococcus cannot accomplish this task on its own due to its complete lack of mobility as it relies on replication to spread to other cells in the body. The T4SS is capable of directed twitching motility when the system comes into contact with the SpA molecules secreted by Staphylococcus aureus. The system shown in Figure 1, is the proposed model of the T4SS system. Here we focus our study to better understand three of the proteins used in this system. Two of the proteins, ChpB and PilH, were both identified through a bacterial two-hybrid assay (Yarrington et al., 2022) as being integral to the directional control of the T4SS system. The third protein, PA0012, is hypothesized to be necessary for the activation of ChpB and PilH in the system.

Discussion

PilH and ChpB were overexpressed and purified for use in protein assays. These protein assays aimed to verify the proposed function and binding partners of these proteins. The proposed function of PilH is as a receiver domain protein which, after undergoing phosphorylation, engages in a conformational change allowing it to act as a coeffector and bind to its potential binding partner PA0012 (AlphaFold model of PilH can be seen in Figure 2). This complex is thought to have an integral role in the extension of the Type IV pili (Yarrington et al., 2022). The proposed function of ChpB is as a methylesterase protein with a receiver domain, potentially capable of undergoing phosphorylation (AlphaFold model of ChpB can be seen in Figure 3). The methylesterase activity supposedly has use in the extension and polymerization of the Type IV pili within the Pil-Chp pathway when ChpB is complexed with PA0012 (Figure 1). A protein crystal of PA0012 and cdG in complex has been created and partially analyzed by Nicholas Hammons for the identification of protein binding sites (Figure 4). Since PA0012 is a PilZ domain protein, it is hypothesized that to become functional, it must first form a complex with the coeffector molecule cdG (McCarthy, 2008). Therefore, Isothermal Titration Calorimetry was conducted on the PA0012:cdG complex to confirm this potential binding activity.

The results of our work allowed us to determine the dissociation constant (KD value), (n=3) and stochiometric ratio of the PA0012:cdG complex shown in Figure 5. The binding properties of phosphorylated and unphosphorylated PilH to its binding partner PA0012:cdG, and the properties of unphosphorylated ChpB to the PA0012:cdG complex were also tested. We also discovered the volatility of PilH and ChpB in storage conditions of -20C, and 4C respectively. As there has been no previous purifications of ChpB in literature, this was also the first successful purification of the ChpB protein.

EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

Bacteria Culture, Strain, and Plasmids The strain of E. Coli is BL21DE3, and the plasmid used was a modified PET28a vector. E. Coli was cultured in 50mL cultures containing 25g/L LB broth and 50g/mL Kanamycin. These cultures were conducted in 250mL Erlenmeyer flasks and were placed into an incubation shaker set to 200rpm at 37C for 18-20 hours. 25mL of the 50mL culture was transferred into each of the two, 1L Erlenmeyer flasks, each containing 1L LB and 50g/mL Kanamycin. These 1L cultures were then returned to the incubation shaker for 2-4 hours. After 2 hours, OD600nm (Optical Density at 600nm) was measured at 30-60-minute intervals until the culture reached 0.6-0.8 absorbance units. At the desired turbidity, the culture was allowed to sit at room temperature for an additional 10 minutes to cool and 1mL of culture was removed for SDS-PAGE analysis.

Protein Overexpression After reaching room temperature, 500L of 1M IPTG were added to each 1L LB culture, resulting in an end concentration of 0.5mM IPTG. This was added to induce overexpression of the protein in the culture. The cultures were then placed into the incubation shaker set to 200rpm at 20C for 4.5 hours. At this point, a 1mL aliquot was taken from the culture for later analysis on SDS-PAGE.

Stopping Point The cultures, after overexpression, were then transferred to different containers for centrifugation. A 500L sample was taken, centrifuged for 2 minutes at 4C and 14,000rpm and then stored for later analysis on SDS-PAGE. The remaining culture was placed into a 4C centrifuge and spun at 5,000rpm for 20 minutes. For long term storage, (i.e., months to years), simply decant the supernatant and freeze the remaining cell pellet at -20C.

ChpB Cell Lysis ChpB cell pellet was thawed and resuspended in 50mL of Buffer A, (50mM

Tris, 250mM NaCl, 10mM Imidazole, 5% glycerol, pH 8 at 4C), and 1 pellet of cOmplete, EDTA-free Protease Inhibitor Cocktail was added to the resuspended pellet. If thawing, resuspend pellet in a 4C fridge or cold room and use a stir plate and stir bar to stir until resuspended. After resuspension, take the solution and pour it into a sonication-safe beaker, and place the new beaker on ice. Use parafilm to wrap the cover to prevent the solution from splashing out of the beaker and cut a small hole for the sonicator probe to stick through. The settings used for sonication were 85% amplitude, 45 seconds per round, pulse for 2 seconds on and 1 second off. The sonicator was run for 6 cycles for the 50mL solution, waiting 2-5 minutes after each cycle for the beaker to cool down and to readjust the sonicator to make sure it was at the right height (the melting ice caused the beaker to sink slightly so readjustments were made after each round of sonication). After sonication, the solution was then transferred to a container for centrifugation. The 50mL solution was centrifuged for 45 minutes at 15,000rpm and 4C. Since ChpB is not a membrane bound protein, it should not be present in the pellet, however, it does interact with membrane bound proteins so there may be a small concentration of ChpB present. A sample of the pellet was collected for SDS-PAGE analysis and the remainder of the pellet was discarded.

ChpB DNA Removal After cell lysis, the cell pellet was discarded, and the supernatant was transferred to another tube. 200L of 1% (v/v%) PEI was added to the 50mL of supernatant. This resulted in a 100L/25mL ratio of PEI to supernatant and a final concentration of 0.004% PEI in the supernatant. The supernatant is then centrifuged at 4C and 15,000rpm for 30 minutes and the new supernatant is then transferred to another tube and the pellet discarded.

For SDS-PAGE analysis, scrape the pellet off the sides of the tube and resuspend it in 25mL Buffer A (50mM Tris, 250mM NaCl, 10mM Imidazole, 5% glycerol, pH 8 at 4C). Vortex to suspend the pellet in the solution.

ChpB NiNTA HPLC/FPLC Protein Purification A 5mL HisTrap column was used for NiNTA purification. Buffer A (50mM Tris, 250mM NaCl, 10mM Imidazole, 5% glycerol, pH 8 at 4C), was used for equilibration and initial wash of the sample. Buffer B (50mM Tris, 250mM NaCl,

300mM Imidazole, 5% glycerol, pH 8 at 4C) was used to elute the sample from the column. Initial Buffer A concentration was 100% at the beginning of elution but gradually decreased a t a rate of ~2%/min as Buffer B was added to the column until Buffer B concentration reached

100%, forming an increasing gradient of imidazole. The wash was conducted at a rate of ~1mL/min to prevent too much pressure from building up and elution rate was conducted at an identical rate. Fractions were collected at 4mL intervals starting from the initial addition of Buffer B to the column. Fractions were collected for SDS-PAGE analysis based on the UV spec data at 280nm. UV spec data was collected at 280nm in all column runs due to the high absorbance values of tryptophan and tyrosine at 280nm. SDS-PAGE and UV Spec data for ChpB and PilH purification can be observed in Figures 6-7.

ChpB Concentration and SEC purification Fractions selected through SDS-PAGE analysis were pooled together and concentrated down to 0.1mL using Amicon Ultra Centrifugal Filter, 30 kDa MWCO and diluted using SEC Buffer (50mM NaPO4, 125mM NaCl, 2mM BME, 1mM EDTA pH 7.0) to reach a final volume of 1mL. The concentrated sample was then loaded onto an HPLC connected to a Superdex 200 Increase 10/300 GL for size exclusion chromatography for

further purification. The SEC column had been equilibrated using SEC buffer before sample load. The flowrate was ~1mL/min and ran for 1.5 hours. This was done to prevent too much pressure from building up in the column. This resulted in buffer exchange, removing the imidazole, and further purifying the sample by size separation. Fractions were collected in 4mL aliquots and UV spec at 280nm was used to select fractions for SDS-PAGE analysis. These fractions were then pooled together and concentrated down to 2mL using Amicon Ultra Centrifugal Filter, 30kDa MWCO and stored in 4, 500L aliquots at 20C. The results for PilH and ChpB SEC purification are shown in Figures 8 and 9 respectively.

PilH Cell Lysis and Purification PilH cell lysis and purification followed the same steps above albeit with a few changes. Colum Buffer A was 50mM Na3PO4 300mM NaCl 10mM Imidazole pH 8.0 and Columb Buffer B was 50mM Na3PO4 300mM NaCl 300mM Imidazole pH 8.0. The SEC buffer was 50mM Tris, 0.5mM EDTA, 2mM DTT, 10% glycerol, 150mM NaCl, pH 7.5 and instead of using the Amicon Ultra Centrifugal Filter 30kDa MWCO filter, the Amicon Ultra Centrifugal

Filter, 3kDa MWCO was needed due to the size of the protein (ChpB was ~40kDa, PilH is only ~10kDa). The rate of columns used was ~2mL/min due to the decreased size of PilH and because PilH was not prone to aggregation like ChpB appeared to be (this is assumed due to the large unexplained peak of high molecular weight seen in Figure 7. It is likely that ChpB formed aggregates, causing this peak to appear).

PA0012:cdG ITC Protocol The cell was rinsed 3x with ddH2O, 3x with SEC buffer, and 2x with degassed SEC buffer before being filled with 2mL 270M PA0012 suspended in degassed SEC buffer. The syringe was washed 3x with dH2O. The ITC was conducted with degassed SEC buffer and the sample cell was filled with 2mL with 270 M PA0012 suspended in degassed SEC buffer. The syringe was filled with 600L of 12.4 M cdG but only 300L was consumed on each run, this was done to allow for easier refilling in subsequent runs. The run parameters were: 21 total injections, 25C cell temperature, 10 reference power, 120 sec initial delay, 4 sec duration, 180 sec spacing, 2 sec filter period. The injection parameters were: 14L vol, 14 sec duration, 240 sec spacing, 2 sec filter period. The run was repeated in triplicate and all run data was collected for analysis. Freeware ITC analysis programs were used, Sedfit, Sedphat, and GUSSI to analyze the data and attain experimental values. The KD , ΔG, ΔH, -TΔS, and n (stochiometric ratio) of the PA0012:cdG complex were determined from this assay (Figure 5). ITC Protocols used were present in two papers and rewritten by lab members within the Dr. Fuentes lab at the University of Iowa, (Brautigam, 2016; Bastos, 2021).

Pull-Down Assay Protocol Several pull-down assays were performed to test the binding properties of PilH and ChpB to the PA0012:cdG complex. The column resin was agitated after the addition of any liquid to allow for better binding to the resin beads. The pull-down assays were conducted with a 1mL HisPur NiNTA Spin Column. 250L containing a total of 200 g of His-tagged ChpB/PilH were loaded onto the NiNTA column and allowed to incubate on the column for 1 hour at 4C in a 2mL Eppendorf tube. After incubation, the column was placed into a new Eppendorf tube and centrifuged at 700xG for 30 seconds. Flowthrough was collected for later SDS-PAGE use. The column was then washed with 200L Buffer A, (seen above in NiNTA purification), 5 times and centrifuged for each wash step. The flowthrough was collected for every wash step. 200L with a total of 200g of PA0012 were loaded onto the column and 5x molar excess of cdG was added and allowed to incubate for 30 minutes at 4C. Following this, 200L Buffer A were used for washing (wash repeated 5x, flowthrough was collected in Eppendorf tubes). Then, 200L Buffer B, (seen above in NiNTA purification), was used for elution of sample, allowed to incubate for 10 minutes and then centrifuged at 700xG for 30 seconds. The flowthrough was collected in an Eppendorf tube. This was repeated for a total of 5 elutions. The collected flowthroughs were then analyzed using an SDS-PAGE.

Phosphorylated PilH Pull Down Assay Modifications Modifications: Due to the short half-life of phosphorylated PilH stemming from its ability to dephosphorylate itself, (possessing phosphatase activity), the initial incubation time was cut down to only 30 minutes. The PA0012 and cdG were incubated prior to their addition to the column. The PilH was incubated on the column with the PA0012:cdG complex for 30 minutes before being washed with Buffer A (washing was conducted five times). After washing, Buffer B was used for elution of the sample (conducted 5 times). The flowthrough from each wash and elution was used in an SDS-PAGE for analysis of binding properties. The results of this experiment are seen in Figure 10. Buffer A and Buffer B for this experiment were done with the PilH specific column buffers described above.

Radioactive Acetylation Phosphorylation Assay Protocol The small molecule phosphate donor (AcP) was radioactively labeled and the solution for radioactive labeling was let to sit for

30 minutes. After labeling, the small molecular phosphate donor was used to phosphorylate the PilH protein. The methods for radioactive phosphorylation were based off a paper published in mBio (Willet, 2011). The phosphorylated PilH was collected at 9 time points, (30 sec, 1 min, 2 min, 5 min, 10 min, 20 min, 40 min, 60 min, 90 min), and graphed in Figure 11. These radioactively labeled samples were then run on an SDS-PAGE and placed into a radioactive phosphor screen for analysis. These data gave us a half-life of ~42.62 min, much longer than the previously identified literature value of 14 min (Silversmith, 2016). Using these phosphorylation values, we were able to determine time-sensitive restrictions and adapt the pull-down assay seen above for optimal results.
